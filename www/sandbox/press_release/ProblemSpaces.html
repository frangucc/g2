Franchino inadvertently started Gamifyed in 2013 as he tried to teach his three children computer science (2nd, 3rd and 4th grade).

“It was impossible. I simply could not get the buy-in amongst my kids. They refused to willingly spend time on it no matter how much I incentivized it. It was too intangible for them.”

Eventually he discovered the reasons why they were reluctant:

  1. It felt like more school work to them. It wasn’t as cool to them as it was to their father.
  2. The digital natives were more interested in playing traditional video games and sports
  3. His kids had no context of why they should spend time studying computer science. The benefits and opportunity were not clear to them.

A programmer, HCI designer and user researcher by trade, Franchino quickly saw the opportunity in this crisis. He began asking other parents what their views were on the matter and found that most parents had similar goals and frustrations, with no solution at hand.

“These kids were born with iPhones and tablets in their hands. They don’t care about how they are made, programmed or what impact the underlying technology can have on their future. They take technology for granted.”



Franchino isn’t just talking about learning to code apps either. He’s talking about ECE, an emerging field that bundles electrical engineering, computer sciences, the internet of things and more.

“Today you can put your skin cells on a chip and analyze them with a Pi. You can remote control a live beetle with diodes and some code. There will be 50 billion IoT objects online by 2020. Even your soccer ball will soon have a MEMS chip and a tiny wifi module that can tell your phone how much force or spin you kicked the ball with.”

Last year Franchino quit his job and began attacking the problem space full-time with heaps of creativity. “I began authoring parts of a graphic novel illustrating the life of a young hacker using the likeness of my oldest son, Frankie Jr. I like to think I combined lots of action with real world hacks and technology. Every chip, hardware part, code snippet and brand in the book is real, nothing is masked.”

Franchino found success in doing this and his kids are now eager to learn on their own and are fascinated with hacking and hardware. “I’ve given the materials to other parents and the results are in, this definitely could work.”

Although entertaining, contextual and easy to read, the graphic novel itself is not enough to actually teach kids to code or work with hardware. They need projects.

Realizing this Franchino put a kit together so his kids could get down and dirty with tech. The goal of the first kit was to provide a small, portable workstation based on the Raspberry PI and Arduino that the kids could put together in minutes and dive right in. That was a year ago. Today Gamifyed has evolved into something much more and is considering the alias Problem Spaces for it’s entire offering. Franchino claims the opportunity is to get kids learning the fundamental science behind the 350 known electrical computer sensors. ie. sonar, infrared, accelerometers or lasers and their respective problem spaces (how and where they are applicable). “I want kids to always have the context in which technology is applicable in mind. ie. Biology => Marine => Oil Spills”

Since gaining traction amongst friends and family Franchino has been focused establishing a real trajectory by keeping it small and controlled. The company has postponed its plans to prematurely launch on Kickstarter stating that it would ultimately become a terrible form of debt.

“When we are ready we will go to market. Launching a premature product will only frustrate kids and/or their parents, which is completely counter-intuitive to our mission.”

“The goal is to get kids to invest a mere 10% of the effort they put into sports or video games.”

Studies have shown that students who can program at any level beyond an intro course in highschool are 80% more likely to graduate with a STEM related degree. There is also the 10,000 hour rule which Franchino agrees is completely accurate.

“Most newbs in college struggle with fundamentals and get overwhelmed by the complexity of programming, hardware and the science behind it all. That’s why 60% of students who start out on a STEM path in college surrender. It takes time and you simply cannot learn this stuff overnight. A good programmer spends at least 10k hours before he becomes a master. You would not expect your child to play professional football if they had never played in high school. Computer science is no different.”

To test out the concepts and products on a broader group of participants, Gamifyed Inc. has scheduled the opening of a Park Ridge based dojo for local grammar and high school students. At the Young Turing Academy students can come in after school, on weekends and throughout the summer to jam out on fun software/hardware/gaming and creative projects.

The goal of the Young Turing Academy is to be the boy scouts of computer science. It’s also how Gamifyed wants to usability test it’s end products such as the Problem Spaces app, it’s Expansion Packs and it’s flagship product, the Node. After these products have been battle tested at the YTA we will begin delivering them to the rest of the world.”

For now Gamifyed says it doesn’t want to be classified as a startup. Instead they want to focus on becoming a profitable small business and learning from their best customers, the kids. “If we can accomplish our goals locally we will try to scale it up. But first, it has to work.”

That said the company is still interested in investment consideration from sophisticated investors who can add value. The Silicon Alley is just getting started and has a bright future, however it will require lots of young talent. Other countries have left us in the dust in science.
