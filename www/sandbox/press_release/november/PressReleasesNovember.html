TechCrunch - November 21, 2015

Meet the company who says they can help better organize the World’s engineering problems.

ProblemSpaces.com claims EE, CS and ECE students are begging for a better way to learn, practice and play with sensors and their code. “MEMS sensors and small bits of hardware are becoming easier to obtain however the way kids study them could be greatly improved,” states CEO Francesco Pinto. “We’re creating what we believe is the ideal environment for students to study this stuff.”

In the wake of Uptakes billion dollar valuation this month, Gamifyed Inc. has quietly been launching problemspaces.com just up the street. It’s an online computer chip and hardware parts store with a clever taxonomy friendly to all ages with a very special community.

“In each field you have many critical problem spaces. For example, in biology you have oil spills. Within that particular space you have what’s known as “the edge of innovation.” It’s where we want to meet our users and join their efforts,” suggests Francesco.

The company is also introducing a game-like tutorial app and two physical products positioned at teenage engineers; expansion packs and a “Smart Object Console” named Higgs for fiddling with electricity, sensors, programming, physics, biotech, robotics and even neuroscience. They’ve even created a comic book they say gives kids a context to how and where ECE will fit into our future.

Both Uptake and Gamifyed seem to be burning the candle at both ends which is interesting because the two companies founders were recently involved in another venture together. An announcement last week from Uptake revealed they are retrofitting Caterpillars heavy machinery with smart sensors to better predict malfunctions while Problem Spaces says they want to teach kids about sensors with an ever inspiring blend of apparatus and content.

The key to getting kids to play with this stuff consistently is the real challenge. “We’ve created a new type of computer and call it a node for short, a term inspired by the field of Wireless Sensor Networks and Electrical and Computer Engineering. We like to think of it as a $200 MIT workbench.”

IoT does seem to be the next major revolution. “It’s as if Electrical Engineering and Computer Science had a baby. Nodes could become the norm in how techs operate in the field or how tech camps teach hardware hacking. You might use a node to mission control one or even hundreds of other sensors. It might make that near space solar weather balloon project more likely or your next biology project a high-tech innovation. It’s portability and power supply allows it to be left in the field, unlike your laptop. In that sense, it becomes a completely wireless and battery powered router.”

Beyond the nerd talk the product does seem really unique and flavorful. We can see how it grabs kids and parents attention. It combines all the core things an up and coming computer science geek could ever ask for.

The company claims it will soon support hundreds perhaps even thousands of unique problem spaces and that those pioneering in ECE will have easy access to parts, plug-and-play code and social support.

“We’ve developed a technology that releases rewards and ships out new expansion packs in the mail based on your child's accomplishments.” It’s important that it’s fulfilling for both the parent and child as parents are the ones who ultimately need to fork up the cash for the high-tech hobby. “The more our users achieve, the more components and swag they acquire. It puts kids backs up against the wall in a field that can be difficult to get real world project experience. It’s the flight simulator for budding engineers with a heavy focus on chips. We’re gonna be the first to gamify hardware education by a landslide.”


Right now everything does seem to be a bit scattered. Tutorials on blogs can be flaky, random instructables lack focus and access to parts, materials and prototyping options all need to be improved.

“Try and find a progressive hardware tutorial on the web - it won’t happen. Codecademy.com and a bagillion other apps offer a tiered and gamified approach to learning web programming because it’s the best way to teach the complex subject. The same goes for Duolingo and their approach to teaching new languages. Why can’t we do this for hardware and sensors, a field that can be even more complex than either of those?”

The tenacious new company also wants to know what challenges students face when trying to prototype or market their inventions to their teachers, colleagues or future employers.

Youth focused companies in this category such as Adafruit, LittleBits and SparkFun are exploding because of their user friendliness, customer support and approach. “We think we can compete in a healthy way given our background in gamification, marketing and distribution. We want to sell our competitors parts too! Lady Ada deserves mad respect and most of her parts are made in the US, in NYC. She’s a diety right now doing tons of good for the field. Her services have helped us get to where we are today.”

After catching a sneak peak of Problem Space’s interactive three.js tutorials we must say the company's flavor of content is incredibly visual however they are still early, something they say their investors are attracted to.

When asked the specifics about their new investors they declined to comment and said, “It’s easy for smart business people and investors to overlook what we are doing because it’s complex, but we like that - so we’re looking for smart capital who understands our end goal of delivering hands on ECE education to kids. If our initial direction even somewhat works it could get viral and social and addicting, fast, possibly as much as video games and apps.”

To help organize themselves Gamifyed Inc. has brought in some of the finest PHD’s and data geeks they say they have ever had the opportunity to work with. Located in Chicago’s Fulton district near the new Google building and at a small office in San Francisco, the company is seeing a huge surge in resumes from top graduates who say the problem we are trying to solve is very real. The company says they offer extremely competitive packages for their new hires and that everyone is treated much like a co-founder at this point.

“The company is firing on all cylinders trying to hit some new plateaus. If we bend this next corner we will get established. We completed our march to 50k one week after our coming out party and we expect to reach the sale of 100 nodes per day by December.”




The company says the question they can’t answer just yet is: what will happen once people get their hands on the node. What might they do with it that we’re not expecting. It’s a totally new form factor and it’s a Linux computer with wifi and optional cellular connectivity.”

Last week the company announced the joining of a key advisors and a data science lead, Rajiv Shah (Data Scientist, PHD, Researcher, Policy and EE multiple degrees), Thomas DiChiara (Neuroscientist, Northwestern) as well as Samuel Basset (political scientist, generalist and school teacher). Frank is also confident he’ll get his previous advisors the former chair of the Department of Computer Science at Georgetown University and current Professor of Biostatistics, Bioinformatics and Biomathematics in the Georgetown University Medical Center.

“We are working with several Fellows of the AAAS, ACM, IEEE, and NAI and can help lead us in the right direction. All of our advisors are trying to clear the path in-front of us.”

Of the 15 major study categories, engineering has the highest median earnings, yet less than 20% of students choose a STEM path. Of those students, 40% of students can’t hack it and drop out.

In 2009, U.S. scientists fielded nearly 29 percent of research papers in the most influential journals, compared with 40 percent in 1981. The STEM crisis is causing a reduction in research, which restricts growth. Even more alarming, 36 percent of teachers in the US aren’t qualified to teach it and still do.

With a strong and very active advisory board Frank says problemspaces.com and their new products are seeing a massive amount of interest from some of the finest students, which he says is extremely validating, because they are highly representative of their best customer. He says they all want one thing, way more projects for their resume.



Frank definitely fits the profile of an oddball geek that thinks he has what it takes to lead a new instructable platform to the promised land. You can immediately sense how hardened he is as entrepreneur and he seems incredibly in control of his venture. He goes on to state that he feels a very real empathy for young inventors who struggle getting their ideas going, graduate or non. “I’ve been there,” he adds, “many times.”

With an industry expected to grow from 11 to 50 billion by 2020 there seems to be no shortage of theory on the big picture at Gamifyed however for now it’s heads down for the company who says they are laser focused on backing up their value proposition and on manufacturing a kick-butt product.

The company says it will be profitable before it raises any new capital and that its two flagship products, the Expansion Pack Line and the Smart Object Console are already flying off the shelves. Avanzato, our incubator, has been really good to us and we recommend talking to them if you have an IoT project you’re trying to commercialize, however it’s time for us to soar. “Graduating our first major incubator will be a bitter-sweet moment for us.”

“People are reserving 5-10 at a time and are willing to pay up-front for this so we’ve chosen Kickstarter as our debut marketing platform for our total company launch this November. We’ve already manufactured nodes in small runs and are in the refinement stages of our developer preview model so we think we’ll be able to ship developer preview nodes within 60 days after the Kickstarter. Our expansion packs are already shipping and can be used without a node.”

Higgs bridges some important things together and makes it more convenient to work on projects, more often. One might even call it a glorified Raspberry Pi and Arduino case. If the platform as a whole takes off we’ll all be wondering why it hadn’t happened sooner.

Gamifyed and ProblemSpaces aren’t the only ones in town touting a AoT device. We are new and there is a lot we need to find out. There is validation in the market confirming that nodes could start showing up everywhere around us, even at an enterprise level.

Recently in Chicago mayor Rahm Emanuel has agreed to begin installing hundreds of smart nodes on light poles to perform what is known as urban sensing as part of his new technology plan. Smart nodes can collect dozens of types of sensor data, from noise pollution to environmental conditions while publishing the data to the cloud for further analysis.

We’ve sold over 500 units to people coming in and out of our offices and are already receiving 50 orders per day online. “Let there be no mistake. When you see this thing, especially in person, you will reach for your credit card, immediately. You’ll want one for you, your nephew and little Timmy, the nerd down the street poised to be the next Bill Gates.”

“We had to shut down all our ads for expansion packs because we we’re getting so many orders so fast and we weren’t prepared.”

Parents don’t seem to be playing around when it comes to wanting their kids to learn to program. The company says it’s greatest challenge is cracking the code with kids and how they spend their free-time. “When my kids come home they want to play video games and sports or hang out with friends. Our field studies led us to a 4.5” robotic cube with expansion packs that play like games. Kids also want to unlock accomplishments in exchange for vanity and recognition when they work hard at something.”

Beyond that the node is a funky little customizable Linux machine that kids can train, trick out and mod. It can be woken up from the phone, fits in your backpack and ships with an AI personality named Higgs that kids can program. The cube is covered in LED lights and has been intentionally left rugged looking for what the company considers, nerd swagger.

The company says it plans to keep everything as open source as possible and that creating apps for the node will be a wide open territory early on with very few restrictions. Frank is known for being a bit wild in his approach to starting companies and has let go of a few early on for reasons he’d rather not mention. He’s known for saying some off the wall whacky stuff, for speaking fluent Italian and for wearing gold teeth to work. He is clearly the embodiment of a millennial CEO. In many ways, he’s the anti CEO.

Local eCommerce Czar Brian J Wasz joins ProblemSpaces as Co-Founder, just in time.

"Brian adds a wealth of eDistribution experience. His previous business fulfilled and sold goods to Groupon, Woot, Tanga, NoMoreRack and hundreds of other online retailers. The goal at ProblemSpaces appears to be the omnichannel Radio Shack isle for small electrical computing hardware and sensors. In less than a month, Brian has already helped us grow the inventory by 1500 SKUS and we're ready for pick and pack."

ProblemSpaces CEO Frank Pinto and Brian Wasz's back story is rich and colorful. "We started Dillyeo together in 2007 and got the business up and running. After a couple years, Frank moved on," explains Brian. "In 2010 Frank was offered the opportunity to help Groupons venture capital firm Lightbank grow their portfolio and I think his decisions to do that have put him in the position he's in now, at just 33. We're both excited to be working again under one roof."

Frank has built more than a dozen companies for others. His claim to fame is that he started a daily deal business before Groupon, helped a dozen startups at Lightbank and got his first million from Eric Lefkofsky and Brad Keywell in 2012. "Looking back I'm not sure how I even got my foot in the door," jokes Frank. "But I'm glad I did, because I learned a lot from people who were like Gods to me. My dudes over there have grit, and I might be the grittiest person ever. We're like jackrabbits in Chicago."

"I sold Dillyeo to Brian in 2010, confident Brian would take it over, but I didn't think he'd take the business to such new heights. The time has come for us to merge back our businesses and transition into something way more scalable than simply drop shipping and flipping. Everything we have learned over the years will be put to the test by ProblemSpaces.com.

The partnerships with the both Brian and Avanzato will guarantee a steady pipeline of customers, manufacturing oversight and parts procurement. With a touch of creativity on the content side we plan to bundle up Expansion Packs with a social gamification strategy. We think this might be a new paradigm for learning ECE, a booming new field that too many drop out of because they got involved too late."

Expansion Packs are akin to really good plugins for software which follow the convention over configuration paradigm coined by 37Signals' Jason Fried and DHH, and are ultimately managed, by community, much like Gems, NPM or Bower packages. Bundling up heir hardware is what Brian has joined the company to focus on. They are ready now, we have them all prepared and are waiting for orders as of today, November 1st. Soon, others will be able to create them as well. We literally live with our customers.

"We want our customers spending more time tweaking their inventions and bringing them to life as opposed to just trying to get the devices powered or work properly. Our user research has shown that context, vanity and stimulation is key. With Expansion Packs, you unpack, complete the circuit and the payoff immediately starts rolling in. The only thing you need to do as a user is tweak the logic or make actions and callbacks to trigger other behaviors - we are two steps ahead of you as our user. This means that developers can make closed loop expansion packs for kids or enterprise that are extremely interactive. On blogs and instructable websites, this stuff is implemented really poorly, and it's not as social as you might think.

If we can perfect this at scale, this could be a billion dollar enterprise. I don't think people understand we sell a 4" cubed Linux workstation with an onboard microcontroller and breadboard, high resolution TFT screen and infinite expansion capabilities. It's technically an open source Robot that can handle physical and virtual tasks. These small nodes pack a punch in terms of performance and ship with a personality named Higgs, a persistent mentor that can be trained by it’s owner.Our package manager is heavily integrated with APIs such as Githooks, IFTT and Zapier, so I'm confident we're ready for press and to bring people aboard, especially developers. We've only publicized the physical side thus far, when we take our stand next month, we'll unveil much more about our open source SDK."

The demand for what ProblemSpaces is offering has been good despite not having done any marketing yet. We are on our way to 50k, swimming with the current in a the IoT maker space, a sector expected to grow from 11 to 50 billion over the next 5 years. We can't go wrong.

Brian was also the companies first money-in angel, so he's been familiar with the operation since inception. With Brian on board the company we will be able to put to work dozens of relationships, trade secrets, distribution expertise and sourcing skills."
